Introduction: The vantage, the questions, the method

A claim to test, not a creed to defend.

The view from the other shore

Imagine the world economy not from London or New York but from a beach on the Malabar coast, or the deck of a dhow running before the monsoon from Aden to Gujarat. It is a deliberately awkward place to stand, because almost every long-run history of the world economy is written from somewhere else — from the North Atlantic, looking outward, with Europe as the active party and the rest of the world as the stage on which European energy plays out. Tell the story that way and its shape is foreordained: the world is poor and static until, around 1500 or 1750, Europe stirs, and modern prosperity radiates from a single source.

Stand on the Indian Ocean shore instead and a different shape appears. You notice that for most of recorded history the largest, richest, most commercially sophisticated economies were in Asia — Han and Song China, the Abbasid caliphate, Mughal India — and that the flow of the world’s most coveted store of value, silver and gold, ran toward them, not away. You notice that Europe spent long centuries as a periphery: an exporter of slaves, furs and raw metal, an importer of manufactures and ideas, paying for what it wanted in bullion it could not itself produce in quantity. And you notice that the period in which Europe genuinely ran the world economy is short — concentrated in the century or so after 1750 — and that even at its height it rested on the Asian periphery it governed.

This book takes that view seriously enough to build a whole history around it. But the vantage point is an engine, not a slogan. The argument it generates is a claim — and the job of the book is to test that claim, period by period, against what the evidence will actually bear, and to be candid about where the evidence is thin and where serious scholars disagree.

The spine: the claim we are testing

Here is the argument in one paragraph. It is worth reading twice, because the rest of the book is an extended interrogation of it:

Viewed from the Indian Ocean, the world economy spent most of its history centred on Asia — visible in the direction bullion flowed and in where the world’s goods were made. Integration advanced and retreated in waves shaped by climate, disease, technology and money, along a durable maritime channel and a periodically resurgent overland one. Europe’s dominance was real and transformative but bounded — concentrated in the century after 1750, built partly on the Asian centrality it overturned, and resting at its height on the Indian Ocean periphery it governed. Its timing and depth remain debated, and the East’s aggregate return — measurable but partial, achieved within a Western-built order — is the long arc closing, not simply repeating.

Call this the spine. Its most provocative move is to treat European dominance as an interruption rather than a destination — but a bounded interruption, calibrated rather than triumphalist. Four qualifications keep that honest, and they recur throughout the book:

  1. the interruption was consequential — roughly two transformative centuries, not a blip;
  2. its timing and depth are genuinely open — the crux of The Great Divergence: the centre moves west, where the Great Divergence is debated;
  3. the eventual return east is partial — aggregate weight, not a restoration of per-capita parity; and
  4. it is system-dependent — the Asian re-rise happened within a US-anchored order.

We do not settle the spine in this Introduction. We assemble the tools to test it, and we return to weigh it in the Conclusion: How strong is the spine?.

The four questions

The book asks the same four questions of every period. They are how each chapter is organised, and they are the analytic toolkit the book is trying to hand you. Together they answer the spine — where is the centre of gravity, and is it moving? — without pre-judging it.

ImportantThe four motivating questions

Q1 — Direction. Is the system integrating or disintegrating? (Two refinements the evidence forces on the simple cycle: integration breeds fragility — the links that carry goods carry shocks; and disintegration has winners — a fracturing core can loosen the grip on a periphery.)

Q2 — Channels. Which routes carry the integrating weight — maritime or overland — and are they complements or substitutes? (The maritime Indian Ocean is the durable artery; the overland corridor had one true climax, the Mongol Pax of The high-medieval boom and the Mongol climax.)

Q3 — Modes. Which of the four flows — goods, technology, people, capital — is doing the integrating? Here lies the book’s key analytic move: treat bullion as a good until the nineteenth century, then as money/capital after — and use its direction as the running tracer of the centre of gravity.

Q4 — Europe. What is Europe’s role — peripheral, entrant, dominant, one of several cores — and how does it change? Asked as an open question, not assumed.

The spine is answered jointly by Q3 (follow the bullion, then the capital) and Q4 (Europe’s changing role); Q1 and Q2 describe how the system that holds the centre is wired at each moment. The arc the four questions trace, chapter by chapter, is this:

no single centre (Ch 1) \(\to\) Asia the production-centre of a multipolar relay (Ch 2) \(\to\) Asia central, Europe peripheral (Ch 3) \(\to\) Asia central, a first north-west-European seed (Ch 4) \(\to\) Asia the silver sink (Ch 5) \(\to\) Asia the workshop, Europe armed (Ch 6) \(\to\) the hinge: the centre moves west (Ch 7) \(\to\) peak Western dominance on an Indian-Ocean base (Ch 8) \(\to\) the order cracks within the West (Ch 9) \(\to\) the return toward Asia (Ch 10).

The bullion tracer, and why it flips

The reason to follow bullion is that, before modern capital markets, metal moved toward the workshop. People who made things the rest of the world wanted — Indian cotton, Chinese silk and porcelain — were paid in silver and gold, which therefore pooled where production was most valued. So in antiquity Roman silver drained east to India (The first world system: Rome was the customer, Asia the workshop); in the early-modern centuries the New World’s silver flowed east to China (The world becomes one — and stays Asian). The tracer even works in reverse where it should: the Abbasid core paid silver north to its slave-and-fur periphery (Maritime apogee: Baghdad and Guangzhou, not Aachen), mirroring Rome.

After the nineteenth century the logic flips, because bullion’s economic role changes. Under the classical gold standard and integrated capital markets, gold becomes money/capital, and following it leads not to the workshop but to the financial core — London, then New York. But the book insists you note who underwrote that core: India’s colonial “drain” helped finance Britain’s payments deficits (The first global economy: integration on colonial terms). Sourced in the periphery, intermediated by the core.

Six threads that run the length of the book

Beyond the four questions, six cross-cutting threads are set up here and paid off later.

  1. Centre of gravity — the spine itself, tracked with an explicit evidence ledger entry and a stated confidence level in every chapter’s verdict.
  2. Measurement of success: quantity to quality. This is the book’s quiet antidote to a recurring error. Before about 1800, like every other species, humans turned prosperity into numbers — more people, more total output, bigger empires (aggregate, extensive growth). Only after about 1800 did any society turn it into sustained income per head (per-capita, intensive growth — the escape from the Malthusian ceiling, uniquely modern). The thread is set up in Deep structures: a world without a centre, made the centrepiece of the silver story in The world becomes one — and stays Asian and Companies, coercion and the contest of the oceans, and resolves at The Great Divergence: the centre moves west: the Great Divergence is this switch. It is what stops us reading every “thriving” empire — Han, Song, Mughal, Qing — as if it were growing in the modern sense.
  3. Follow the silver — the early-modern operationalisation of the bullion tracer (The world becomes one — and stays Asian to The Great Divergence: the centre moves west): the same American silver met different monetary conditions and so had different fates — Asia absorbed and specialised, the Ottomans inflated, West Africa debased and depleted, and Europe converted its monetary “loss” into armed-trade and financial power — the bridge to the divergence.
  4. Measurement of integration — how we know. The evidence available to measure integration itself improves over time: from the mere diffusion of goods and ideas, to bullion flows, to wages and prices, to GDP, to a computed centre of gravity. Before prices, only the diffusion prong exists — which constrains what Deep structures: a world without a centre and The first world system: Rome was the customer, Asia the workshop can claim.
  5. Forces. Four drivers sit behind Q1’s direction — climate, disease, technology, policy — two of them natural, a reminder that the world economy has always been embedded in a natural system.
  6. Data survival and bias. What survives reflects record-keeping conventions and the accidents of climate and preservation. Absence of data is not absence of activity, and the surviving record systematically under-documents the very Indian-Ocean world this book centres on. Whose eyes we are seeing through is itself a running question.

A note on method, and on honesty

Two methodological commitments follow from all this, and they are why the subtitle is data and debates.

Data. The book leans on numbers — trade volumes, bullion flows, real wages, output shares, the computed economic centre of gravity — because numbers are how you adjudicate a claim like the spine rather than merely asserting it. The figures and tables are reproducible: generated from datasets you can download and re-run. But the book is equally insistent that the data thin out dramatically as you go back, and that they are biased toward the literate, fiscal, European end of the record. A chapter on the Bronze Age and a chapter on the twentieth century are not equally evidenced, and the book will not pretend otherwise.

Debates. Where the scholarship is genuinely contested — when the Great Divergence began and why; whether interwar disintegration was man-made or inevitable; when “globalisation” really started — the book stages the disagreement rather than smuggling in a verdict. Disputed figures are presented as teaching points, not silently resolved. The aim is not to tell you what to think about the world economy, but to hand you the questions, the evidence and the live arguments, and let you test the spine yourself.

NoteHow to read the chapters

Each chapter opens by following one object that carries its period’s story, locates the period on the arc, sets out the cast and how we know, narrates the period on its own terms, then reads it through the four questions, delivers a centre-of-gravity verdict with a confidence level, and hands off to the next. Look out for the recurring Data exhibit and Debate, and the Follow the money and How we know callouts.
