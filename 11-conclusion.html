Conclusion: How strong is the spine?

Preliminary draft — under review. Published for review; content, figures and citations may still change.

Now the evidence is in, re-weigh the bounded-anomaly claim.

The book opened by refusing to assume that Europe was the protagonist of world economic history. It asked instead a plainer question: where did the world economy’s centre of gravity sit, and was it moving? Ten chapters later the evidence is in, and the answer can be stated in a single line. For most of the arc the centre sat in Asia; there was a real but bounded European interruption, concentrated in the century or two after 1750; and the centre has begun, in aggregate, to return. The task of this conclusion is not to retell that story but to weigh it: to ask how strong a version of the “European dominance as anomaly” reading the evidence will actually bear, now that the whole record can be seen at once.1

1 Sources: the opening question and the four-questions framework set out in the Introduction; the long-run reading synthesised across the ten chapters. Read more: Maddison (2007), Contours of the World Economy; Frank (1998), ReORIENT.

The method held

The instrument that produced that answer was a set of four questions, asked of every period in turn. Was the world economy integrating or disintegrating (direction)? Through which routes, maritime or overland (channels)? Which of the flows, goods, technology, people or capital, was doing the integrating (modes)? And what was Europe’s role, peripheral, central or dominant (Europe)? The four were not a checklist but a triangulation. Two of them located the centre, and two described the system that held it: following the bullion and then the capital found the centre’s position, and reading Europe’s role tracked it from the other side, while the direction of integration and the choice of channel described how the system carrying the centre was wired at each moment. Asked together, period after period, the four questions answered the spine without ever having to assume it.2

2 Sources: the four motivating questions (direction, channels, modes, Europe) and their joint resolution of the spine, developed across the chapters from the Introduction onward. Read more: Findlay & O’Rourke (2007), Power and Plenty.

3 Sources: the “how we know” ladder (diffusion, bullion flows, wages and prices, GDP, the computed centre of gravity) and the quantity-versus-quality (aggregate-versus-per-capita) thread, both running from the Introduction through to Chapter 10. Read more: Broadberry (2021) on long-run GDP measurement; Maddison (2007), Contours of the World Economy.

Running underneath the four questions were two measurement choices that did quiet but decisive work. The first was a ladder of evidence: the book could only ever know the centre as well as its sources allowed, and those sources improved as the story advanced, from the diffusion of crops and techniques in the deep past, to the direction bullion flowed, to wages and prices, to national accounts, and at last to a centre of gravity that could be computed outright. The reading grew firmer as the evidence thickened, which is why the modern verdict is the most secure in the book and the ancient one the most tentative. The second choice was about what “success” meant: aggregate or extensive growth, the kind that shows up as more people and more total output, against per-capita or intensive growth, sustained income per head. That distinction, introduced in the first chapter and carried the length of the book, is the standing antidote to mistaking a big economy for a rich one, and it returns at the end as the crux of the whole verdict.3

The centre of gravity, chapter by chapter

Set out as one movement, the ten verdicts make a single shape. There was no centre to speak of in the first chapter, only a multipolar world of riverine cores and steppe corridors. By the second, Asia had become the production core of a relay that ran from the Mediterranean to the China Sea, with Rome the consumer, India the fulcrum and China the maker, and the bullion drained east to pay for the goods. Through the third and fourth chapters Asia stayed central and Europe peripheral, a margin that exported slaves and raw materials and imported manufactures and silver. In the fifth the oceans joined into a single market, yet the silver that joined them flowed east, to the Chinese economy that priced it highest. In the sixth Asia was still the workshop, but Europe was now armed, the chartered company giving it the means to organise the oceans while Asia kept the production.4

Then the centre moved. The seventh chapter was the great turning point, the period in which Britain industrialised and the centre of gravity crossed to the West, and it was also the moment the meaning of growth changed, from the extensive prosperity every society had pursued to the sustained rise in income per head that was new. The eighth chapter was the peak of that Western dominance, but a peak that rested on the Indian-Ocean periphery it governed, financed in part by a colonial drain running from the edge to the core. The ninth was the order cracking, and the crucial point is where it cracked: the centre did not move to Asia but shifted within the West, as financial leadership passed from London to New York. The tenth was the return, the aggregate centre swinging back east, though only partway and inside an order the West had built. Read these verdicts in sequence and the arc is unmistakable; it is laid out entry by entry in Appendix C.5

Two features of that shape are worth holding onto, because they are what keep it honest. The first is that the line is not a simple pendulum swinging east and west. The centre’s pull in the third chapter ran north, from the Abbasid core to its slave-and-fur periphery, not east; and the decisive move of the ninth ran within the West, London to New York, not across to Asia. A centre of gravity can move inside a region as readily as between regions, and a reader who flattens the arc into a clean east-west oscillation loses both of those turns. The second is that the confidence behind the entries is uneven by design. The plateau of the early chapters is drawn from inference; the dip and the return are drawn from prices, trade shares and, at the very end, a computed coordinate. The shape is firmest exactly where the modern argument needs it to be, and faintest in the deep past where the modern argument does not rest.6

The four questions, resolved across the whole arc

Walked end to end, each of the four questions yields a finding that no single chapter could have produced.

On direction, the long run was a sequence of waves, integration and disintegration alternating: the Bronze Age world, the Rome-Han system, the Abbasid-Tang high tide, the Mongol climax, the silver-welded sixteenth century, the nineteenth-century globalisation and the post-war reglobalisation, each punctuated by a collapse, a fade, a pandemic or a slump. But the waves were not a smooth cycle, and two refinements keep the rhythm from becoming a platitude. The first is that integration bred fragility: the same connectivity that carried goods carried shocks, so that the linked palace economies fell together in the Bronze Age, the Black Death rode the Mongol routes, and the influenza of 1918 and the Depression spread along the steamship lanes and the gold standard. The second is that disintegration had winners: the unwinding of 1914 to 1945 wrecked the core but loosened the colonial periphery’s subordination, and India’s interwar industrialisation and Japan’s surge were seeded in exactly that wreckage. Direction, in other words, never tells the whole story on its own; it has to be read together with who gained and who lost from each turn.7

Read more: Findlay & O’Rourke (2007), Power and Plenty; James (2002), The End of Globalization.

8 Sources: the durable maritime channel and the single Mongol overland climax; the land-to-sea reorientation of Chapter 3 and the Mongol Pax of Chapter 4; channels as complements and occasional substitutes. Read more: Findlay & O’Rourke (2007), Power and Plenty.

On channels, the maritime Indian Ocean was the through-line of the whole book, the durable artery along which the centre’s weight was carried for two thousand years. The overland corridor had a single genuine climax, the Mongol Pax of the fourth chapter, and otherwise ran secondary, with a late and faint echo in the modern revival of the land routes that lay largely beyond the period. The structural set-piece was the land-to-sea reorientation of the third chapter, the moment the sea became dominant for the rest of the pre-modern era. The two channels were mostly complements, bulk and low-value goods by sea and high-value goods by land, but they were substitutes at the margin, each able to take weight from the other when one was disrupted: the swing to the sea after Talas, the Mongol overland surge, the choice of Suez over the Cape. “Which channel?” was always, at bottom, a question about which route carried the integrative weight at a given moment, and why the other was being chosen against.8

On modes, all four flows ran throughout, but the dominant and measurable one shifted, and the single most useful analytic in the book was the change in what bullion was. Before roughly the nineteenth century, silver and gold were traded goods, commodities that responded to arbitrage and flowed toward the economy that valued them most, so that following the metal located the workshop: east to India and China in antiquity and again in the silver age, north to the Abbasid periphery in between. After the nineteenth century, under the gold standard and integrated capital markets, bullion became money and capital, and following it located the financial core instead, London and then New York. The tracer logic flipped with the role. And the flip carried a sting in its tail. Under the colonial order the periphery financed the core, the drain running from India to Britain; under the modern order the arrow reversed, and the rising East financed the established West, recycling its surpluses into the debt of the consumers who bought its goods. Same tracer, opposite direction, and the reversal is one of the sharpest things the long view reveals.9

Read more: Eichengreen (2008), Globalizing Capital; Findlay & O’Rourke (2007), Power and Plenty.

10 Sources: the staged trajectory of Europe from peripheral entrant (Chapters 3–5) through armed challenger (Chapter 6) and hegemon (Chapters 7–8) to one of several cores (Chapters 9–10). Read more: Maddison (2007), Contours of the World Economy.

On Europe, the answer the book set out to test rather than assume comes back staged and clear. Europe was peripheral through the Abbasid and high-medieval centuries, a margin exporting slaves and raw materials. It was a minor armed entrant in the sixteenth century, grafting itself onto a still-Asian silver circuit. It was an organising, armed challenger by the seventeenth and eighteenth, with the means but not yet the production. It was dominant from the turning point through its 1850-to-1914 peak, though its dominance rested on the periphery it governed. And after 1945 it became one of several cores again, as the centre’s weight began to swing back east. Periphery, entrant, challenger, hegemon, one core among several: Europe’s role was never fixed, and the trajectory is exactly the kind that lets its dominance be read as a real but bounded passage rather than the natural order of things.10

The verdict: how strong is the anomaly?

So how strong is the claim the whole book has been testing, that European dominance was an anomaly in a longer Asian-centred history? The honest answer is that the claim is defensible but qualified, a real but bounded interruption rather than either an illusion or a permanent reordering. On the aggregate fact the confidence is high, and for once it does not rest on inference: the centre of gravity can be computed for the modern decades, and the computation drifts unmistakably east. Set against the long span, with China and India holding something between a half and three-fifths of world output to about 1700 and the Western economies reaching roughly half by 1950, the arc reads cleanly. Asia central, a European interruption, the beginning of a return. That is the strongest support the evidence offers for treating the European centuries as, in the long view, an interruption.11

11 Sources: the high-confidence aggregate return (Chapter 10); Quah’s computed centre of gravity; the long-run output shares (China and India about half to three-fifths of world output to about 1700, the West about half by 1950). Read more: Quah (2011); Maddison (2007), Contours of the World Economy.

12 Sources: the four qualifications on the European interruption: consequential (~200 years), timing-contested (the Chapter 7 divergence-dating debate: Pomeranz/Allen versus Broadberry), partial (aggregate not per-capita), and system-dependent (built on the US-led order), drawn together in Chapter 10. Read more: Pomeranz (2000), The Great Divergence; Broadberry (2021); Quah (2011).

What stops the claim short of a clean restoration is a set of four qualifications, each of which the chapters earned. The interruption was consequential, not a blip: it ran for something like two transformative centuries and remade technology, institutions and living standards in ways that did not unwind when the aggregate weight shifted back. Its timing and depth remain genuinely open, and that openness is the crux: if the divergence was late and contingent, as Pomeranz and Allen argue, the interruption was shorter and the anomaly stronger; if it was early and structural, as Broadberry’s evidence suggests, the interruption was longer and the anomaly correspondingly weaker. The return is partial: it is a return in aggregate weight, in manufacturing and total output and the computed centre, and not, or not yet, in income per head, where China and India remained well below the Western level. And the return was system-dependent: it happened inside a US-anchored order, on the institutions of Bretton Woods and the World Trade Organization, on the shipping container, and on American consumer demand financed in the end by Asian savings. A return within a Western-built system is not the same thing as a resumption of the world before 1800.12

The second of those qualifications deserves a sentence of its own, because it is where the book’s verdict is least settled and most honest. The whole anomaly reading pivots on the seventh chapter, and the seventh chapter is the one the book deliberately leaves open. How anomalous the European interruption looks is not something the long-run data can decide on their own; it depends on a contested question about when and why the divergence began, and that question is still live in the literature. The book’s verdict is therefore conditional by design: strong if the divergence was late and contingent, weaker if it was early and structural, and in either case real. To pretend otherwise, to resolve the seventh chapter in order to firm up the tenth, would be to buy a cleaner conclusion at the price of the honesty the rest of the book has tried to keep.13

13 Sources: the deliberately open divergence-timing debate of Chapter 7 as the point on which the strength of the anomaly reading turns. Read more: Pomeranz (2000), The Great Divergence; Allen (2009), The British Industrial Revolution in Global Perspective; Broadberry (2021).

The workshop returns; the income gap persists

The thread the book has carried furthest now closes, and it closes on the difference between two kinds of success. Before about 1800, like every other species, human societies turned good times into more of themselves, more people, more total output, larger empires; only after about 1800 did a few economies learn to turn good times into sustained income per head, the uniquely modern escape from the Malthusian ceiling. That distinction was set up in the first chapter, made the centrepiece of the silver age, where a flourishing Asia turned out to be extensive and involutionary rather than rich per head, and made literal at the divergence, which simply was the switch from the first kind of success to the second. The rebalancing of the final chapter returns the first kind and not, so far, the second. Asia is again the heaviest part of the world economy by total output; it is not yet the richest by output per head, and the aggregate is large in good part because the populations are large. The workshop has returned. The income gap has not closed. Holding those two facts together, without letting either crowd out the other, is the single most important habit the book has tried to teach.14

14 Sources: the quantity-versus-quality (aggregate-versus-per-capita) thread from Chapter 1, through the silver age (Chapters 5–6) and the divergence (Chapter 7), closing at the partial return of Chapter 10. Read more: Hsieh & Klenow (2009) on the productivity gap; Maddison (2007), Contours of the World Economy.

When did the world really turn?

One question the book parked along the way can now be answered. The conventional break in the twentieth-century story falls in 1945, with the end of the war and the building of a new order. But the evidence assembled here points to a later turn. 1945 was the monetary reset, the moment the institutions were rebuilt; the real reglobalisation, and the genuine recentring of the Indian Ocean, came around 1971 to 1973, when the dollar was cut from gold, the oil price quadrupled, the major currencies floated, and the shipping container spread. Read with the ninth chapter’s lesson that disintegration had winners, the Asian re-rise looks less like a clean post-war beginning than like a long process with interwar roots, running through the closure of the 1950s and 1960s to the reopening of China in 1978 and India in 1991. The book leans, on its own evidence, to dating the turn to the early 1970s rather than to the war’s end.15

15 Sources: the parked periodisation question (1945 versus 1971–73), resolved toward the early 1970s in Chapter 10, read together with the “disintegration has winners” finding of Chapter 9. Read more: Findlay & O’Rourke (2007), Power and Plenty; Frieden (2006), Global Capitalism.

What the very long run teaches

Three lessons survive the particular periods that produced them. The first is that integration was a policy outcome and not a ratchet. The first globalisation was undone between 1914 and 1945, deliberately, behind tariff walls and inside imperial blocs, which means that the openness of any age is a choice that can be reversed and not a permanent achievement. The second is that the centre of gravity can move within a region as well as between regions, the lesson of the ninth chapter’s London-to-New-York shift, and a reader who only watches for movement between continents will miss half of what happens. The third is the one the quantity-quality thread keeps alive: “rich” and “big” are different questions, and the same body of data can be made to answer either, so that what looks like a story of triumph or decline often turns on nothing more than which measure is in the foreground.16

16 Sources: integration as a reversible policy outcome (Chapter 9); the centre moving within a region (Chapter 9); the quantity-versus-quality distinction across the arc. Read more: James (2002), The End of Globalization; Findlay & O’Rourke (2007), Power and Plenty.

17 Sources: the three closing questions handed forward from Chapter 10: per-capita convergence, the possible revival of the overland channel, and multipolarity versus a new single centre. Read more: Quah (2011); Baldwin (2016), The Great Convergence.

The book ends, as it began, with open questions rather than a closed verdict, and three in particular it hands to the reader. Will the aggregate return pull a convergence in income per head behind it, or will the gap prove the limit of the rebalancing rather than a stage on the way through it? Will the overland channel, faint and late in this account, thicken into something that matters again, reviving the land routes that the sea displaced a thousand years ago? And is the next century multipolar, several cores and no single centre, as in the world of the first chapter, or does the weight settle into a new single centre, as it did under Europe and then America? The evidence in this book does not decide any of the three. What it does is supply the frame in which they can be argued well, which is the most a long-run history can honestly claim to do.17

The arc, closing

Viewed from the Indian Ocean, the world economy spent most of its history centred on Asia, visible in the direction bullion flowed and in where the world’s goods were made. Integration advanced and retreated in waves shaped by climate, disease, technology and money, carried along a durable maritime channel and a periodically resurgent overland one, by flows of goods, technology, people and capital. Europe’s dominance was real and transformative but bounded, concentrated in the century after 1750, built partly on the Asian centrality it overturned, and resting at its height on the Indian-Ocean periphery it governed. Its timing and depth remain debated, and the East’s aggregate return, measurable but partial and achieved within a Western-built order, is the long arc closing, not simply repeating.18

Cross-cutting questions

These questions span several chapters; each chapter points forward to the ones it feeds. They are written to reward the four-questions toolkit and the live debates, not recall.

On the centre of gravity

  1. When does the centre of gravity shift, and can it shift within a region (London to New York) without moving between regions? Use Chapters 8, 9 and 10.
  2. How strong a version of “European dominance as anomaly” can the full body of evidence support? Hold the divergence-timing debate of Chapter 7 against the partial return of Chapter 10. (The book’s final adjudication.)

On following the money

  1. “Follow the bullion” became “follow the capital.” Compare the eastward silver flow of the silver age (Chapters 5–6), the colonial drain of the high-imperial period (Chapter 8), and the modern recycling of Asian savings into the Western core (Chapter 10). When does “follow the money” locate the same kind of centre, and where does the analogy break down?
  2. Why does it matter that bullion was a good before the nineteenth century and money/capital after? Trace the consequences for what “following the metal” reveals (Chapters 2, 5, 8, 10).

On quantity and quality

  1. When is it legitimate to call a heavy economy a rich one, and when only a big one? Build the answer from the aggregate-versus-per-capita evidence across Chapters 2, 6, 7 and 10.
  2. “The workshop returns; the income gap persists.” In what sense has the centre returned to Asia, and why does the distinction between aggregate weight and income per head decide the answer?

On integration, fragility and direction

  1. “Integration is not a ratchet.” Use the interwar reversal (Chapter 9) and the post-war reglobalisation (Chapter 10) to assess how far, and how fast, an integrated world economy can come apart and be put back together.
  2. “Integration breeds fragility, and disintegration has winners.” Defend or qualify both halves, drawing on the Black Death (Chapter 4), the 1918 pandemic and the Depression (Chapter 9), and the periphery’s interwar turn (Chapters 9–10).

On channels

  1. The sea was the durable artery and the overland route had a single climax. Assess the land-to-sea reorientation of Chapter 3 and the Mongol Pax of Chapter 4, and say when the two channels were complements and when substitutes.

On data and bias

  1. The first chapter’s problem was survival bias; the last chapter’s is the choice of what to measure. How does what we can measure frame what we conclude about where the centre of gravity sits? Use Chapter 1 and Chapter 10, and the rising-confidence pattern of Appendix C.
