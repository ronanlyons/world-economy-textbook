## 1. The chapter's section topics (what the readings must cover)

From the story spine (`module_design/topic_03_story_spine_2026-06-19.md`) and stress-test:

1. **Two cores + the ocean between** — the Abbasid core, Tang→Song China, India as the workshop
   between them, Srivijaya at the strait, a new northern periphery (Rus/Vikings/Khazars), Tibet
   as the land counterpoint, Carolingian Europe at the margin.
2. **The making of an Islamic sea** — Justinianic Plague → Arab conquests → the Abbasid commercial
   zone → Talas (751) and An Lushan (755–63) → the land→sea swing → the maritime apogee.
3. **Follow the silver — and it runs north** — channels (sea ~20× cheaper than land); money
   (the bimetallic dinar/dirham system, credit instruments); the dirham flow north to the
   fur-and-slave periphery; the gold:silver ratio deep dive.
4. **Knowledge & technology as a flow** (added in the stress-test) — paper west of Talas, the
   translation movement, Hindu numerals; the "Arab Agricultural Revolution" as a live debate.
5. **The verdict** — bipolar Asian/Islamic centre of gravity, tested via city size; quantity vs
   per-head income held over to T7; the institutions case study; the Hansen "what triggered the
   surge?" debate.

Two through-questions for the readings: (a) **how big, really?** the move from qualitative
diffusion evidence toward the first quantitative wage/GDP/urban series for this world; (b)
**why did the Middle East later fall behind?** — the "long divergence" literature reaches back
into exactly this golden age, so the chapter can plant the question it pays off in T7.

## 2. Current / recent research (economics & quantitative economic history)

This is the layer thinnest in the existing deck corpus (which is history/archaeology-heavy) and
the one the chapter most needs to feel like a *world-economy* text. All verified this pass unless flagged.

### 2a. Wages, prices, GDP — the first quantitative series for this world
- **Pamuk & Shatzmiller (2014)**, "Plagues, Wages, and Economic Change in the Islamic Middle East,
  700–1500," *Journal of Economic History* 74(1): 196–229. **DOI 10.1017/s0022050714000072** ✅
  — *the* headline quantitative paper: real wages of unskilled workers and GDP-per-capita estimates
  for medieval Egypt and Iraq; wages held *above* subsistence for much of the era (the Justinianic
  Plague and Black Death as demographic levers). Directly supplies the "how big / how rich" thread.
- **Shatzmiller (2022)**, "Structural Change and Economic Development in the Islamic Middle East
  700–1500: Population Levels and Property Rights," *Scottish Journal of Political Economy* 69(1):
  4–22. **DOI 10.1111/sjpe.12277** ✅ — population trends and property rights behind the high
  early-Islamic living standards.
- **Shatzmiller**, work on **prices/price formation** and **human-capital formation in medieval
  Islam** (a "knowledge economy" reading) — several articles; ❓ pin exact venue/DOI before citing.
  Pairs with the stress-test's knowledge-and-technology-flow slide.

### 2b. Markets and institutions in the Abbasid core
- **van Bavel, Campopiano & Dijkman (2014)**, "Factor Markets in Early Islamic Iraq, c. 600–1100 AD,"
  *Journal of the Economic and Social History of the Orient* 57(2): 262–289.
  **DOI 10.1163/15685209-12341349** ✅ — relatively unrestricted markets in land, labour and capital;
  deep urban occupational specialisation (659 recorded job types); strong monetisation. The
  micro-foundation under "Baghdad as the world's largest city."
- **van Bavel (2016)**, *The Invisible Hand? How Market Economies Have Emerged and Declined Since
  AD 500* (Oxford University Press). ⚠ — early Islamic Iraq as the lead case study in a markets-rise-
  then-self-undermine thesis; a structural counter-frame to the "golden age" story.
- **Kuran (2003)**, "The Islamic Commercial Crisis: Institutional Roots of Economic Underdevelopment
  in the Middle East," *Journal of Economic History* 63(2). **DOI 10.1017/s0022050703001840** ✅
  — partnership/inheritance law and the long stagnation of firm scale; the origin point of the
  "long divergence" question.
- **Kuran (2011)**, *The Long Divergence: How Islamic Law Held Back the Middle East* (Princeton UP). ⚠
  — the book-length statement (waqf rigidity, partnership dissolution); flag the live critiques
  (Hanafi flexibility) as debate, not settled.
- **Rubin (2010)**, "Bills of Exchange, Interest Bans, and Impersonal Exchange in Islam and
  Christianity," *Explorations in Economic History* 47(2): 213–227. **DOI 10.1016/j.eeh.2009.06.003** ✅
  — why a shared instrument (the bill of exchange) stayed personal-network-bound in the Islamic world;
  ties to the spine's ṣakk/suftaja credit case study. See also Rubin (2011, *Economic Journal*, ❓ DOI)
  and **Rubin (2017)**, *Rulers, Religion, and Riches* (Cambridge UP) ⚠.
- **Chaney**, "Religion and the Rise and Fall of Islamic Science" (working paper, rev. 2023) ❓
  and **Blaydes & Chaney (2013)**, "The Feudal Revolution and Europe's Rise: Political Divergence
  of the Christian West and the Muslim World before 1500," *American Political Science Review* ❓
  (verify vol/DOI) — political-economy accounts of when/why the Islamic world's lead eroded; useful
  as the forward-looking "hold the question" cross-ref to T7.

### 2c. Trade, geography and persistence (the world-economy / spatial angle)
- **Jha (2013)**, "Trade, Institutions, and Ethnic Tolerance: Evidence from South Asia,"
  *American Political Science Review* 107(4): 806–832. **DOI 10.1017/s0003055413000464** ✅
  — medieval Indian-Ocean trading ports (Muslim shipping + Hindu producers as complementary,
  non-replicable services) leave a thousand-year institutional imprint. The cleanest "Indian Ocean
  trade had long-run economic consequences" paper for the chapter.
- **Copeland**, "Riding the Monsoon: Geography and Iron Age Trade in the Indian Ocean" (CEPR /
  cliometrics working paper, c.2021–22) ⚠ working paper — a gravity model of pre-modern Indian-Ocean
  trade built on the monsoon; quantifies the "geography did the rest" claim (the monsoon + chokepoints).
  Period is slightly earlier (Iron Age) but the method/finding generalise to the chapter's channels Q.
- **Bosker, Buringh & van Zanden (2013)**, "From Baghdad to London: Unraveling Urban Development in
  Europe, the Middle East, and North Africa, 800–1800," *Review of Economics and Statistics* 95(4):
  1418–1437. DOI 10.1162/rest_a_00284 ⬚ — the city-size dataset behind the centre-of-gravity proxy
  (Baghdad/Córdoba/Chang'an dwarf Aachen). The live-deck exercise dataset.
- **Reba, Reitsma & Seto (2016)**, "Spatializing 6,000 Years of Global Urbanization," *Scientific
  Data* 3: 160034. DOI 10.1038/sdata.2016.34 ⬚ — the alternative urban dataset (FigShare) for the
  same exercise.

### 2d. Money and the northern silver flow (numismatic economic history)
- **Kovalev & Kaelin (2007)**, "Circulation of Arab Silver in Medieval Afro-Eurasia: Preliminary
  Observations" ❓ (verify venue/DOI) — the quantitative case that millions of Abbasid/Samanid
  dirhams moved north; >1,350 silver deposits, Baltic/Volga concentration. The hard backbone for
  "follow the silver — and it runs north." (Use alongside the spine's hoard counts.)
- **Recent dirham archaeometry** (Oxford/Diva-portal, 2021–22): "The scale of dirham imports to the
  Baltic in the ninth century" ❓ — lead-isotope sourcing of Viking-age silver, sharpening *when* and
  *from where* the silver flowed. Optional, for the methods/"how we know" thread.
- **Ross & Bettenay (2023)**, "Gold and Silver: Relative Values in the Ancient Past," *Cambridge
  Archaeological Journal* 34(3): 403–420. DOI 10.1017/S0959774323000355 ⬚ — the gold:silver-ratio
  deep dive (regionally plural → converging band; Islam fuses a gold-west and silver-east).

## 3. Classic readings & historians on trade and economic issues

The deep-bench history, much already in the deck `refs.bib` (⬚). Grouped by the chapter's structure;
*east → west* per house style.

### 3a. Indian-Ocean / maritime syntheses (the spine)
- **Abu-Lughod (1989)**, *Before European Hegemony: The World System A.D. 1250–1350* (OUP).
  DOI 10.1093/oso/9780195067743.001.0001 ⬚ — the polycentric world-system frame (closing edge of T3).
- **Hourani (1951; rev. Carswell 1995)**, *Arab Seafaring in the Indian Ocean and Mediterranean*
  (Princeton UP). ⚠ — the classic on Arab/Persian sailing to Tang China; Belitung now confirms it.
- **Chaudhuri, K. N. (1985)**, *Trade and Civilisation in the Indian Ocean: An Economic History from
  the Rise of Islam to 1750* (Cambridge UP) ⚠ — and **Chaudhuri (1990)**, *Asia Before Europe* ⚠.
  The foundational economic history of the ocean; opens exactly at "the rise of Islam."
- **Beaujard (2019)**, *The Worlds of the Indian Ocean*, Vol. 1 (Cambridge UP) ⚠ — IO-as-a-system,
  use selectively (very long). Standing reference for T1–T5.
- **Pearson (2003)**, *The Indian Ocean* (Routledge) ⬚⚠ and **Alpers (2014)**, *The Indian Ocean in
  World History* (OUP) ⬚⚠ — short accessible syntheses (already in deck refs).
- **Margariti (2007)**, *Aden and the Indian Ocean Trade* (UNC Press) ⚠ — the Red-Sea/Aden node as
  the Gulf route's rival; pairs with the Fatimid Red-Sea shift at the chapter's close.
- **Curtin (1984)**, *Cross-Cultural Trade in World History* (Cambridge UP) ❓ — the classic
  trade-diaspora frame (Radhanites, Arab–Persian merchants as one commercial language).

### 3b. The Islamic core economy (the distinctive economic-history bench)
- **Lombard (1975)**, *The Golden Age of Islam* (orig. *L'Islam dans sa première grandeur*, 1971) ⚠
  — the classic economic history of the Abbasid commercial florescence (money, towns, trade routes).
- **Ashtor (1976)**, *A Social and Economic History of the Near East in the Middle Ages* (Collins) ⚠
  — the standard older economic-history survey; dated on some numbers but foundational.
- **Udovitch (1970)**, *Partnership and Profit in Medieval Islam* (Princeton UP) ⚠ — the classic on
  Islamic commercial/credit institutions (qirad/mudaraba); the historian's counterpart to Kuran/Rubin.
- **Goitein (1967–93)**, *A Mediterranean Society* (5 vols, on the Cairo Geniza, UC Press) ⚠ — the
  unmatched documentary window on 11th-c. Mediterranean/IO Jewish merchants; the chapter's closing
  "Geniza lights up the Indian Ocean" hand-off. (Greif's Maghribi-traders work builds on this — a
  bridge to the institutions literature.)
- **Watson (1974/1983)**, *Agricultural Innovation in the Early Islamic World* — JEH 1974 article
  DOI 10.1017/S0022050700079602 ⬚, book 1983 ⚠ — the "Arab Agricultural Revolution"; teach as the
  live debate with **Decker (2009)** DOI 10.1353/jwh.0.0058 ⬚ and **Squatriti (2014)**
  DOI 10.1017/S0022050714000904 ⬚.
- **Bulliet (2009)**, *Cotton, Climate, and Camels in Early Islamic Iran* (Columbia UP) ⬚⚠ —
  the cotton boom (the ocean's new signature textile) and climate.
- **Hoyland (2015)**, *In God's Path: The Arab Conquests and the Creation of an Islamic Empire*
  (OUP) ⬚⚠ — the political formation of the core.

### 3c. China & the eastern core / the land counterpoint
- **von Glahn (2016)**, *The Economic History of China: From Antiquity to the Nineteenth Century*
  (Cambridge UP). DOI 10.1017/cbo9781139343848 ⬚ — Tang/early-Song money, prices, commercialisation,
  the southward demographic shift. The standing China reference (T3–T7).
- **Lewis (2009)**, *China's Cosmopolitan Empire: The Tang Dynasty* (Harvard/Belknap).
  DOI 10.2307/j.ctvjnrtgk ⬚ — the Tang quantitative anchor (Chang'an, the 742 census).
- **Sen (1996)**, "The Administration of Maritime Trade during the Tang and Song Dynasties,"
  *China Report* 32(3): 251–278. DOI 10.1177/000944559603200302 ⬚ — the shibo-shi/shibosi maritime
  superintendency (the institutional counterpart to the Abbasid fisc; surfaces the under-told eastern pole).
- **Beckwith (2009)**, *Empires of the Silk Road* (Princeton UP). DOI 10.1515/9781400829941 ⬚ — the
  Central-Eurasian/land frame; **Beckwith (1987)**, *The Tibetan Empire in Central Asia* (Princeton UP) ⚠
  — Tibet as the land power contesting the Silk Road.

### 3d. Europe peripheral — the Pirenne debate (cross-ref ECU3303)
- **Pirenne (1939)**, *Mohammed and Charlemagne* (Allen & Unwin) ⚠ — the thesis that Islam pushed
  Europe's economy inward/north; "Europe peripheral" is the Pirenne reading.
- **McCormick (2001)**, *Origins of the European Economy: Communications and Commerce, AD 300–900*
  (Cambridge UP). DOI 10.1017/cbo9781107050693 ⬚ — the great quantitative rebuttal/refinement
  (coin finds, communications): European commerce revived *through* this peripheral trade
  (slaves north→south for silver). Peripheral ≠ stagnant — the chapter's honest caveat.
- **Wickham (2005)**, *Framing the Early Middle Ages* ❓ and **Wickham (2023)**, *The Donkey and the
  Boat: Reconstructing the Mediterranean Economy, 950–1180* ❓ — the source of the "sea ~20× cheaper"
  and "~1–2% export share" figures; the most recent heavyweight on the Mediterranean economy at the
  chapter's close. (Verify DOIs/editions.)

### 3e. The northern silver periphery & popular syntheses
- **Frankopan (2015)**, *The Silk Roads* (Bloomsbury) ⬚⚠ — accessible narrative of the routes
  (use with care: the squirrel-fur figures are popular-history colour — see stress-test fix #7).
- **Hansen (2020)**, *The Year 1000* (Viking) ⬚⚠ — the framing trade book and the "what triggered the
  surge c.960–1000?" live debate (surplus→population→trade vs the commercial-revolution accounts).
- **Brook (2018)**, *The Jews of Khazaria* (Rowman & Littlefield) ⬚⚠ — the Khazar/steppe-edge node
  on the silver road north.

### 3f. Maritime archaeology (the hard proxy)
- **Krahl, Guy, Wilson & Raby (eds.) (2010)**, *Shipwrecked: Tang Treasures and Monsoon Winds*
  (Smithsonian) ⚠ and **Flecker (2001)**, "A Ninth-Century AD Arab or Indian Shipwreck in Indonesia,"
  *World Archaeology* 32(3): 335–354. DOI 10.1080/00438240120048662 ⬚ — the Belitung wreck; Flecker
  (2010) revises the build to Gulf-Arab (stress-test fix #1).
